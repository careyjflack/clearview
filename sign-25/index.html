J.C. Bush Grocery Store

Grocery & market
Owners
Julius Caesar "J.C." Bush (1904 – 1970)
Dates
c. 1935 – 1970
Affiliated
Shirley Nero, Normores, Withers Cudjoe, Joe Golden, Hattie Lou Oliver, Mack French

There are few other locations that stand out in the minds of Clearview residents as much as the J. C. Bush Grocery and Market. Townsfolk fondly recall walking over to the store as kids on their lunch break from school and spending fifteen cents to purchase a pop and either peanuts or a bologna sandwich on crackers.

Originally from Kentucky, the Bush family relocated to Oklahoma in 1901 before settling in Clearview by 1910. The J. C. Bush store was one of three grocery stores operated by the Bush brothers: Charlie, Julius, and Langston. Initially opened in the mid-1930s as a joint venture between Julius and Langston Bush, the J.C. Bush store would become a distinct business at this current location. Like many of the other proprietors in Clearview, J. C. Bush renovated a residential building to function as a grocery store.

Over the years he would expand the store, build a porch, add double glass to the storefront, and install streetlights to brighten the area around the store (possibly to ward off potential burglars). Men would gather on the porch to tell stories, or 'lies', play checkers, or listen to the Dodgers game on the radio. Julius Bush could be found among the crowd on the porch until a customer needed to be serviced. Inside there would be glass counters on either side with penny candies and household goods to the right and the butcher area and refrigerators to the left, as well as wall shelves lined with canned foods on both sides. Julius Bush also stocked the Black Dispatch, a weekly African-American newspaper published in Oklahoma City, and a news box where townsfolk could leave their news to be featured in the paper. Julius Bush lived in the back of the store along with his wife, Violet. They ran the store for approximately 35 years until his death in 1970. In addition to being a grocer, Julius Bush was also a dedicated civic leader in the community. He was a school board member, voting registrar, election official, 4-H Club advisor, and superintendent at the Church of Christ church across the street.

The J. C. Bush Grocery Store was a commercial and social pillar of the community that represents the self-sufficiency and entrepreneurship found in Clearview and other all-Black towns during and after segregation.

L.H. Haynes Telephone Office

Telephone office
Owners
L.H. Haynes (1878 – 1956)
Dates
Dates unknown

From a 3/2/1956 obituary in the Okemah News Leader:

"Masonic Rites for L.H. Haynes, 78, pioneer Clearview resident who died at his home, will be at 2 p.m. Sunday at the Tyree Methodist church. Interment will follow in the Clearview cemetery under direction of the Hyde Park funeral home. Haynes, a native of Mississippi, came to Clearview in 1907 and was a rural mail carrier there for many years. He also taught school for several years, served as a minister in the Methodist church, and was active in civic movement. Survivors include his wife, two daughters, Mrs. Rosie Swain and Mrs. Borsey Henderson, both of Clearview, and one son R. H. Haynes. There are also two brothers, Clemans Haynes, Oakland, Miss. and W. H. Haynes, Enid; and tow grandchildren. Pallbearers will be Earl French, J. P. Owens, Fred McMillan, Tom Withers Jr., R. Reynolds, and Ollie Walker."

W.L. Bush Grocery

Grocery
Owners
William L. Bush (1892 – 1984)
Dates
1935 – 1984
Affiliated
Shirley Nero, Normores, Withers Cudjoe, Joe Golden, Hattie Lou Oliver

The W. L. Bush store was one of three grocery stores operated by the Bush brothers: Charlie, Julius, and Langston. Initially opened in the mid-1930s as a joint venture between Julius and Langston Bush, the W. L. Bush Grocery store would become a distinct business at this current location. Like many of the other proprietors in Clearview, Langston Bush renovated a residential building to function as a grocery store.

Langston and Julius Bush's stores offered similar goods although some residents recall that Langston served more of the professional people in town such as schoolteachers. Langston didn't play checkers or serve bologna sandwiches and possibly offered some higher end items. What made his store stand out is that it had one of the first telephones in Clearview. He would let anyone use the phone and would go around town delivering messages at people's homes. Langston Bush lived in the back of his store for several years until moving into a separate home in town and permitting Dessie Forshee to operate a barbershop in the store in 1952.

Langston was a long-time school board member, sponsor of the Miniature Golf Course, vice-president of the Clearview branch of the NAACP, and was active in veterans organizations. The W. L. Bush Grocery store is another example of the thriving entrepreneurship and civic engagement in Clearview during the mid 1900s.
