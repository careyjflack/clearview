Jeff Horn Grocery Store

History

Born in Louisiana in 1875, Jeff Horn migrated to Clearview and eventually opened two different grocery stores.

He purchased one lot in 1920 and sold the store and its contents in 1925. Horn died in 1981.

Foster Liggon's Grocery Store

History

Foster Ligons, born in 1890, operated one of Clearview's early grocery stores at this location after moving into the former Jeff Horn grocery store.

More than a businessman, Ligons was a community leader who worked at Clearview High School and served on the Clearview School Board. His name appears on the cornerstone of the WPA gymnasium, now the Clearview Community Center, a reminder of his role in supporting local education and public life.

Ligons died in 1972 and is buried alongside his wife in Clearview Cemetery, linking his story to the generations of families who helped build and sustain the town.

J.C. Bush Grocery Store

History

There are few other locations that stand out in the minds of Clearview residents as much as the J. C. Bush Grocery and Market. Townsfolk fondly recall walking over to the store as kids on their lunch break from school and spending fifteen cents to purchase a pop and either peanuts or a bologna sandwich on crackers.

Originally from Kentucky, the Bush family relocated to Oklahoma in 1901 before settling in Clearview by 1910. The J. C. Bush store was one of three grocery stores operated by the Bush brothers: Charlie, Julius, and Langston. Initially opened in the mid-1930s as a joint venture between Julius and Langston Bush, the J.C. Bush store would become a distinct business at this current location. Like many of the other proprietors in Clearview, J. C. Bush renovated a residential building to function as a grocery store.

Over the years he would expand the store, build a porch, add double glass to the storefront, and install streetlights to brighten the area around the store (to ward off potential burglars). Men would gather on the porch to tell stories, or "lies," play checkers, or listen to the Dodgers game on the radio. Julius Bush could be found among the crowd on the porch until a customer needed to be serviced.

Inside there would be glass counters on either side with penny candies and household goods to the right and the butcher area and refrigerators to the left, as well as wall shelves lined with canned foods on both sides. Julius Bush also stocked the Black Dispatch, a weekly African American newspaper published in Oklahoma City, and a news box where townsfolk could leave their news to be featured in the paper.

Julius Bush lived in the back of the store along with his wife, Violet. They ran the store for approximately 35 years until his death in 1970. In addition to being a grocer, Julius Bush was also a dedicated civic leader in the community. He was a school board member, voting registrar, election official, 4-H Club advisor, and superintendent at the Church of Christ church across the street.

The J. C. Bush Grocery Store was a commercial and social pillar of the community that represents the self-sufficiency and entrepreneurship found in Clearview and other all-Black towns during and after segregation.

W.M. (Wash) Irving Dry Goods

History

William "Wash" Irving was an enterprising and pivotal figure in the foundational years of Clearview, Oklahoma. Operating a prominent general merchandise store right on his town lot, Irving boldly positioned his business as a local powerhouse, claiming to hold the absolute largest inventory in town while confidently maintaining that he could undersell any of his competition.

His essential role in establishing Clearview's early commercial pipeline was cemented in local history by The Clearview Patriarch, which famously documented that Irving had received the town's very first shipment of freight in 1912. Through his competitive business model and deep-rooted community presence, he helped shape the early commercial landscape of the town.

Second Post Office

History

The first U.S. Post Office was established September 30, 1902. In 1904 an order was issued by the U.S. Postal Department changing the name Clearview Post Office to Abe Lincoln Post Office, but was rescinded a month later, retaining the original name.

John Grayson was named the first postmaster. Those who succeeded him were N. H. Starks, M. W. (Wash) Irving, Mary Doggett, Mattie E. Lucas and Imogene Chilton Bruton. From the Clearview Post Office, the outlying area was served by a rural route carrier. The first rural route mail carrier was Ellis Washington, followed by O. J. Simms, L. H. Haynes, J. P. Owens, Robert (Bob) Burden, and Jimmie Burden. J. P. Owens served as rural route carrier from 1927 to 1974.

During the early 1970s the Clearview and Weleetka routes were consolidated due to a vacancy in the Weleetka rural delivery service, moving the Clearview carrier to the Weleetka office. Postal service from the Clearview office was continued until November 17, 1992 when the Postal Department discontinued service from the office after ninety years. However, mail service was provided to the area by the rural carrier from the Weleetka Post Office along with rural route and cluster box service.

L.H. Haynes Telephone Office

History

Luphus Haynes was a pioneering resident who came to Clearview from Mississippi in 1907. He was a rural mail carrier, teacher and principal, minister, cafe owner, and between the 1920s–1930s he managed a telephone office on Main Street and laid the lines that would connect Clearview and its residents to other towns.

He operated the Mill & Elevator, the Telephone Exchange Co., and the Clearview Cafe in the Martin old stand that would later become Langston Bush's grocery store. He also managed the telephone office on Lot 51 at one time.

According to James P. Owens, town historian, they would go into the woods and cut holes to make the telephone lines and then extended the lines out in the country for about three miles. The phones were old wall phones in which someone had to turn the crank to make a call. His daughter, Rosie Haynes Swain, is listed as an operator in the 1930 census.

L. H. Haynes would eventually relocate to Oklahoma City and pass away in 1956. His ability to operate a local telephone system underscores the resourcefulness of Clearview residents and the range of services they were able to provide to the town.

Doster Restaurant

History

Mrs. H. Dorster came to Clearview from Boley to open the Mid-Way Cafe on Main Street in 1927.

She unfortunately passed away roughly a year after opening the restaurant, leaving behind a husband and two sons. This building would later be occupied by L.H. Haynes' telephone company and W.L. Bush's grocery store.

W.L. Bush Grocery

History

The W. L. Bush store was one of three grocery stores operated by the Bush brothers: Charlie, Julius, and Langston. Initially opened in the mid-1930s as a joint venture between Julius and Langston Bush, the W. L. Bush Grocery store would become a distinct business at this current location. Like many of the other proprietors in Clearview, Langston Bush renovated a residential building to function as a grocery store.

Langston and Julius Bush's stores offered similar goods although some residents recall that Langston served more of the professional people in town such as schoolteachers. Langston didn't play checkers or serve bologna sandwiches and possibly offered some higher end items. What made his store stand out is that it had one of the first telephones in Clearview, a unique feature carried over from the former telephone office which operated out of the building. He would let anyone use the phone and would go around town delivering messages at people's homes.

Langston Bush lived in the back of his store for several years until moving into a separate home in town and permitting Dessie Forshee to operate a barbershop in the store in 1952. Langston was a long-time school board member, sponsor of the Miniature Golf Course, vice-president of the Clearview branch of the NAACP, and was active in veterans organizations.

The W. L. Bush Grocery store is another example of the thriving entrepreneurship and civic engagement in Clearview during the mid-1900s.

Dr. M.C. Alford Drug Store

History

Referred to as "Clearview's Greatest Citizen" in his obituary in the Black Dispatch, Dr. M.C. Alford was a long-time drug store operator and civic and business leader in Clearview. Originally from Mississippi, he was born to a slave master father and formerly enslaved mother and was sent to Indian Territory in 1902 with the hope of a better life.

He eventually settled in Clearview where he built and operated Clearview Drugs, the only drugstore in town, from 1908 until his death. Dr. Alford received his medical training from Meharry Medical College, a private historically Black medical school, and went into the drugstore business after realizing he didn't like practicing medicine. He partnered with Drs. J. H. C. Lucas and Scott to fill the prescriptions written by the other two physicians.

Beginning in the 1920s he began offering burial services in town including burial, caskets, and transportation. Dr. Alford was also well-known for his civic involvement through the Clearview Commercial Club, the Clearview Burial Association, the school board, and donating the construction of a new church in Clearview.

His keen business sense enabled him to acquire property and oil releases in several states across the South through the Black Cow Oil, Gas and Mining Co. of Clearview and he accumulated a significant fortune which he intentionally invested back into the community through the purchase of tax resale property located in Black communities, allowing them to recoup lost tax revenue. Dr. Alford's extraordinary life and career embodies the expanding opportunities and success available to African Americans in all-Black towns in the early twentieth century.

Trotter's Cleaning & Pressing & Barbershop

History

W. G. Trotter, who was also a Baptist pastor, opened this cleaning and barbershop in Clearview in the early twentieth century. He also had a company truck that his employees would use to deliver and pick up clothing.

Not much is known about the barbershop, but it's possible that Ronderter A. Cobb would've been cutting hair there in the early 1930s.

M.M. (Blanks) Alford Home

History

Prominent Clearview resident Major C. Alford lived in this brick home in the early twentieth century. Born in 1880 in Mississippi, he owned the drugstore and funeral home.

After the death of his first wife he would later marry Martha M. (or M.J.) Blanks Alford. Mrs. Alford was previously married to Nat Blanks, who she operated the N. & M.J. store with. In 1918 an unfortunate event occurred wherein Martha killed her husband in an act of self-defense. She would later marry Dr. Major C. Alford.
