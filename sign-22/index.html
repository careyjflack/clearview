Alonzo Talton's Grocery Store

History

Born in 1869, Alonzo Talton moved to Clearview in 1912 and opened his grocery and mercantile store.

For nearly a half century the Taltons served the town and surrounding communities with most of their needs from clothes, tools, groceries, and other items. Items for sale included unground coffee, sewing thread, long johns, and overalls.

Alonzo Talton retired in 1951 and passed away a few years later in 1957 at the age of 89 at an Okmulgee nursing home. In 1992 fire destroyed the mercantile store building. The long mercantile table which held many of the goods was moved to the "old school gym" and is in use by the community during community functions.

Sam Clark & Charlie Bush Grocery Store

History

This lot was home to a succession of stores and cafes that operated from 1903 through the 1950s. Located on freedmen land originally allotted to George Jackson, his son Lemuel opened the Abe Lincoln Trading Company in 1903 the same year Clearview (then Lincoln) was incorporated.

Its location alongside the western side of the Ft. Smith and Western railroad tracks made it an ideal site to operate, selling goods and building materials to new residents as they settled in the newly incorporated town.

The building had several owners and notably was reopened as a grocery store by Sam Clark, an early Clearview resident and farmer from Louisiana, around the start of World War I. He operated the store for several years before relocating to Bristow by 1930.

Charlie Bush took over the store in the early 1920s which became the first of eventually three grocery stores operated by the Bush brothers: Charlie, Julius, and Langston. Originally from Kentucky, the Bush family relocated to Oklahoma in 1901 before settling in Clearview by 1910.

Charlie and his wife, Faye, offered meals, dry goods, blocks of ice, ice cream, cold drinks, and fresh groceries and their store was known as ‘the cheapest and most sanitary restaurant in town.’ He would stock his store with locally sourced goods like eggs that patrons would bring in and trade for other merchandise. It’s possible that his mother, Matilda, helped manage the store based on the 1930 census listing her as the proprietor of a cafe.

Charlie Bush also drove a route through the rural African American communities around Clearview as far as Grassy Lake and North Fork, trading and selling groceries from his delivery truck. Known as a fast driver, residents would be ready to look for Charlie Bush’s truck or listen for his whistling before coming out to purchase ice, ice cream, or other goods.

For a brief time in the 1930s residents recall playing slot machines in the store before they were confiscated by the county sheriff. Charlie Bush’s store was considered the best grocery in Clearview and is remembered as a high quality establishment to purchase goods through the highs and lows of World War I, oil booms, the Great Depression, and World War II.

Believed to be the first building built in Clearview, it is still standing and serves but empty.

J.W. Thompson's Law Office & Voter Registration

History

James E. Thompson, born in 1859, made money in mining interests before moving from Anadarko, Oklahoma, to Clearview in 1913. Town founders J. A. Roper and John Grayson had moved out of the town, and Thompson quickly became one of the leaders.

He purchased many of the town lots and surrounding land (he reported that he owned or leased over 5,000 acres of land in and around Clearview), renting them or using the land for people to become sharecroppers. He had a law office and sued for the railroad to erect a depot in Clearview. His wife, Neva, served as the first superintendent of the Clearview School.

James Thompson also promoted the town and attracted more settlers through his townsite company. He financed the Clearview Patriarch newspaper and used it for town promotion as well. He did fight against Jim Crow laws, registering African Americans to vote. He was in a group of Black leaders that traveled to Washington, D.C. and testified to Congress against segregation. Thompson was murdered in 1930.

Masonic Lodge

History

As early as 1904, Clearview had a Masonic organization and a Masonic Temple, evidence that fraternal life was part of the town from its early years. The community also supported the women's branch, the Order of the Eastern Star, which gave Black women an organized role in fellowship, charity, leadership, and mutual aid.

African American Freemasonry traces its roots to Prince Hall and other Black men in Boston, Massachusetts, whose African Lodge became one of the foundations of Prince Hall Masonry in the late eighteenth century. In Black towns and neighborhoods across Oklahoma, Masonic lodges and Eastern Star chapters helped provide the kinds of support often denied by segregated public institutions, including social networks, burial assistance, scholarships, charitable work, leadership training, and community fundraising.

Clearview's Masonic Order built this lodge in 1947, continuing a tradition that had long connected Black civic leadership with fraternal service. Alongside churches and newspapers, fraternal orders were key cogs in the social, political, and cultural life of many early all-Black towns and Black communities in Oklahoma.

H. Haynes Telephone Office

History

Luphus Haynes was a pioneering resident who came to Clearview from Mississippi in 1907. He was a rural mail carrier, teacher and principal, minister, cafe owner, and between the 1920s–1930s he managed a telephone office on Main Street and laid the lines that would connect Clearview and its residents to other towns.

He operated the Mill & Elevator, the Telephone Exchange Co., and the Clearview Cafe in the Martin old stand, Lot 82, that would later become Langston Bush's grocery store. This lot seems to have been another location where he operated the telephone office at one time.

According to town historian James P. Owens, they would go "out in the woods and cut holes to make the telephone lines. And they extended the lines out in the country for about three miles. And they operated on these old wall telephones. You know, you have to come down and turn the crank." His daughter, Rosie Haynes Swain, is listed as an operator in the 1930 census.

L. H. Haynes would eventually relocate to Oklahoma City and pass away in 1956. His ability to operate a local telephone system underscores the resourcefulness of Clearview residents and the incredible services they were able to provide to the town.

Dry Cleaning & Pressing Shop

History

There is not much known about this dry cleaning shop in part because of the multitude of businesses that were operating in Clearview at the time.

There are multiple advertisements of pressing shops in Clearview in the early 1910s including one for the Burks & Lester Cleaning and Pressing Shop which operated on east Main Street, possibly on this lot. There is another advertisement for the OWL Tailor Shop (alternatively titled the Owl Pressing Club) in 1913.

Recreation Center

History

Sylvester Franklin opened the Crackers Tavern (or Bar) on Main Street in 1945. A decade later he would expand his business by opening the Crackers Pool Hall & Recreation Center in 1957 as a place where men could "play pool, checkers, and dominoes" and be served punch and cold drinks.

It was initially in the Owens building and was relocated to the Marks building on this lot later that same year. Open from 8am til noon, it offered a gathering space for men in the morning separate from the vices associated with beer joints and taverns.

Pool Hall & Bar

History

Susie Allen operated the Last Chance Pool Hall & Bar in the former Abe Lincoln building during the 1980s.

By this time there had been a steady population decline as many families moved away for better jobs and the Clearview school had closed. However, many residents remained or returned in retirement and establishments like Last Chance provided social spaces for the community.

Public Water Well

History

In Clearview's early years, indoor plumbing was uncommon. Residents relied on dug wells or hauled water from nearby springs and creeks, sometimes from as far away as Grassy Lake, then stored it in cisterns inside or near their homes.

If the cisterns were not refilled regularly, the water could become stagnant and unsafe. This public water well represented one effort to provide residents with cleaner, more reliable water. Even so, concerns about possible groundwater contamination from nearby oil wells meant that many families continued hauling water from surrounding communities.

Clearview did not receive water lines until 1972, when Okfuskee Rural Water District No. 3 was established and began purchasing water from the City of Okemah.
