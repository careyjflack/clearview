Holland Bakery Shop

History

There is little known about Holland's Bakery Shop besides the knowledge that it operated during the golden age of Clearview in the 1910s–1920s.

Early Clearview resident James P. Owens recalls that it was one of the many businesses along Main Street, surrounded by cafes, confectionaries, and grocery stores. It may have been operated by a Mr. and Mrs. Thomas Hollin(s) who lived in Clearview at the time.

Regardless, this bakery is one of many businesses in town that offered a range of goods and services to residents and underscores the prosperity and opportunity in Clearview in the early twentieth century.

Mayfield Pop Bottling

History

In 1914, The Clearview Patriarch reported that a Mr. Mayfield from Boley, Oklahoma, was considering moving his "Soda Water" factory to Clearview.

The possibility of a bottling business fit well with Clearview's growth during the early twentieth century, when the town supported newspapers, churches, schools, general stores, cotton gins, and other businesses along the Fort Smith and Western Railroad. A soda water factory would have added another locally owned enterprise to the community and may have supplied bottled soft drinks for stores, social gatherings, travelers, and families in and around Clearview.

J. P. Owens later recalled that many different types of sodas were bottled for sale, suggesting that the business produced more than one flavor or variety. Physical evidence of the company survived when Tom Lucas of Clearview found a soda bottle marked with the name Clearview Bottling Company. That bottle is important because it confirms that the town's business activity included not only farm-related trade, but also small manufacturing and commercial food and drink production.

The Clearview Bottling Company reflects the ambition, entrepreneurship, and everyday community life that helped make Clearview one of Oklahoma's notable historic all-Black towns.

Hackney Café

History

Henry Hackney, born in 1893, opened one of Clearview's early cafés, and his wife, Octavia Hackney, born in 1898, helped him operate the business.

In a town built by Black settlers seeking opportunity, independence, and community life, a café would have been more than a place to buy a meal. It likely served as a gathering place where farmers, railroad travelers, laborers, students, church members, and neighbors could exchange news, talk politics, rest from work, and strengthen local ties.

As Clearview grew along the Fort Smith and Western Railroad and supported stores, churches, schools, and other businesses, the Hackneys' café would have been part of the everyday social and economic life that helped the town function. Their partnership also reflects the important role that families, and especially women working alongside husbands and relatives, played in sustaining small-town Black businesses.

Henry died in 1949, and Octavia lived until 1970. Both are buried in Clearview Cemetery, linking their story to the larger history of the families who built, worked, worshiped, and remained connected to the town.

Owens Confectionaries

History

Sampson and Minnie Owens came to Clearview in 1913, and their story stands out because it began not behind a counter, but in the dirt.

Before opening a town business, the couple farmed several acres of Irish potatoes, a crop that required steady labor, careful timing, and confidence that the harvest could be sold or used to support a growing household. In a community often remembered for cotton, churches, schools, and small businesses, the image of the Owens family raising potatoes gives their story a different flavor—one rooted in food, hard work, and the practical business of feeding people.

By the following year, they had turned that same connection to food into a meat market and lunch counter, where customers could buy fresh meat, sit down for a meal, or enjoy ice cream, cold drinks, and confections. Their move from potato fields to lunch counter suggests a family willing to adapt quickly, combining farming, food service, and hospitality in a way that helped meet the everyday needs of Clearview residents.
