Clearview Public School District I-37

Clearview's public school served the town from the early 1900s through 1969 — the WPA gymnasium it left behind still stands as a community center today.

History

Clearview opened its first school in the early 1900s, originally offering grades one through eight. Early town leaders including J. E. Thompson emphasized the importance of all children receiving an education and his wife, Neva, was one of the first principals of Clearview School.

Like other Black schools during segregation, Clearview had to compete with white schools for adequate funding. However, the investment and emphasis on education in all-Black towns, as well as the absence of explicit racial discrimination, served as an incentive for many African Americans who were leaving the South in search of better opportunities.

While there were efforts to establish a high school for many years it wouldn't be until the 1920s that Clearview opened a high school. In 1923, the Rosenwald School Fund oversaw the construction of a six-room brick schoolhouse in Clearview and by the following school year the Douglass School was open. Douglass gradually expanded into a high school over the next couple of years. Before then, Clearview students would have to travel to Okmulgee or Boley to attend the nearest high school, each over twenty miles away. Douglass, which would eventually be renamed Clearview High School, served the town and several surrounding communities from Grassy Lake, Galilee, and North Fork. The educational and commercial ties that brought these rural communities together reinforced social and relational ties to Clearview. Later additions were added to the school including the gymnasium which was built by the Works Progress Administration in 1939 and is still standing today.

Clearview schools offered all the standard courses and a range of extracurriculars including 4-H, New Farmers of America, New Homemakers of America, theater, and more. Teachers were committed to holistically preparing their pupils for the real world, from managing experimental agricultural fields to organizing Black history programs and senior trips to Hot Springs National Park and other places. While some teachers did not live in Clearview full-time, often boarding at Ada Davis' boardinghouse, there was still a sense of shared responsibility between parents, teachers, and the community in ensuring student success.

A testament to the importance of Clearview schools to the community is the fact that the school stayed open over a decade after the 1954 Brown v. Board of Education ruling overturned segregated schooling. The high school was closed in 1964 and the elementary school followed in 1969. While the segregated system inherently disadvantaged Black schools because of inadequate funding, the ability to oversee their children's education from within the community and provide meaningful employment to residents were strong incentives for wanting to keep the school. Instead, Clearview children were bussed to Weleetka to complete their education.

The Clearview community desired to maintain the school facilities and incorporated the Clearview Community Improvement Corporation in order to receive a deed to all of the school property and facilities in the Clearview School District, with the provisions that such property and facilities be maintained, insured, and used for community purposes only. In 1988 the CCIC renovated the old gymnasium to become a community center where residents and visitors continue to gather today.
