Church of the Living God

History

Of the eight churches in Clearview’s early history, the only congregation that still exists is the Church of the Living God. The denomination traces its origins to Mary Magdalena Lewis Tate, often known as “Mother Tate,” an African American holiness and Pentecostal evangelist who began preaching in Tennessee and Kentucky and organized followers into groups known as “Do Rights” before founding the Church of the Living God, the Pillar and Ground of the Truth, in 1903.

The church emphasized holiness, clean living, sanctification, testimony, Bible study, and spirited preaching, and it grew through revivals, assemblies, and traveling ministers. Clearview’s Church of the Living God reflects that broader tradition of Black religious leadership and community-building.

Its building was constructed with stones reused from the demolished Creek Seminole Agricultural College, which once stood on the hill and served the Clearview community from 1914 to 1919. For many years, preachers from Okmulgee and Tulsa traveled to Clearview to deliver sermons.

The congregation usually meets on the first and third Sundays, continuing one of the town’s longest surviving religious traditions.

Blacksmith Shop

History

By 1912, blacksmith shops were essential to everyday life in Clearview, Oklahoma. Founded in 1903 along the Fort Smith and Western Railroad, Clearview had grown into a busy all-Black town with farms, churches, schools, newspapers, general stores, cotton gins, and other businesses serving residents and travelers.

In this setting, C. D.

F. Hulsey operated the ACME Blacksmith Shop on Jackson Avenue.

His work would have supported farmers, teamsters, merchants, and families who depended on horses, wagons, plows, and hand tools before automobiles became common. A blacksmith repaired wagon wheels, sharpened plow points, made or mended metal parts, and shod horses used for hauling crops, traveling to town, and working fields.

Jim Neal also advertised a blacksmith shop across from the train on Hettie Avenue (Main Street), showing how important this trade was to Clearview’s economy. In a community built around farming, railroad access, and local enterprise, Hulsey’s shop represented the practical skill and self-sufficiency that helped Clearview thrive in the early twentieth century.

L.V. Swain Garage

History

Lloyd Swain was a long-time resident of Clearview, arriving as a small child with his family around the time of statehood. He worked on his family farm and must have shown some entrepreneurial and mechanical savvy because he opened up a filling station with West Mayberry in 1926 and began working as a mechanic at an auto garage shortly thereafter.

He worked at the Ford Motor Co. garage in the years leading up to World War II and would eventually take over the Alford Funeral Home Garage, running it as an auto garage.

His mother had migrated from Spain to Mexico before the family moved to Clearview.

Alford Funeral Home Garage

History

The Alford Funeral Home Garage was the location of a family business overseeing the transportation and burial of Clearview residents.

As early as 1928 Dr. M. C. Alford, pharmacist and drugstore owner, began offering burial services in town until his death in 1940. His nephew Willie Alford would eventually take over the business with Pete Golden and in 1959 purchased a Pontiac hearse — also called an ambulance back then — to transport coffins to and from the embalmer in Okmulgee to the church and the Clearview Cemetery. His services were paid for primarily by managing burial policies that residents could purchase.

When a death would occur in the community, the First Baptist Church would ring its bell to let the town know. While potentially not licensed in either handling coffins or driving a car, Willie Alford's business savvy provided meaningful employment for himself and care for the community. Willie Alford sold the business before his death in 1971.

A.L. Gammil Grocery Store

History

Abraham L. Gammill, affectionately known in the community as “Uncle Link,” was born in Mississippi shortly after the Civil War and was raised by parents who had formerly been enslaved.

His life reflected the strength, skill, and determination of African Americans who left the South in search of greater independence and opportunity. Around the time of Oklahoma statehood, Gammill moved west and eventually settled in Clearview in 1916, becoming part of one of Oklahoma’s historic all-Black towns.

In Clearview, he operated both a grocery store and a blacksmith business, two services that were vital to a farming and railroad community. His grocery store likely supplied families with everyday necessities, while his blacksmith work may have supported local farmers, wagons, railroad activity, and the foreman shop.

Gammill was also a dedicated Mason, showing his involvement in the fraternal and civic life that helped strengthen African American communities. He lived a long life and later passed away while residing in a Sapulpa rest home.

His story represents more than personal success; it reflects the resilience, entrepreneurship, and desire for autonomy that drew many African Americans to all-Black towns like Clearview.

Church of Christ

History

Among the many early Clearview churches that served the town was the Church of Christ. The Church was the church Christ established whose members sought to follow the practices of the New Testament church and emphasized local congregational leadership, Bible-centered preaching, baptism, communion, and simple worship.

One of its most recognizable traditions was a cappella congregational singing, and Clearview residents remembered that the church’s songs were always sung without instrumental accompaniment. Services were usually held in the morning, and visiting preachers came from Okmulgee and Tulsa to deliver sermons.

During the spring and summer months, the congregation sometimes held gospel meetings outside under a tree, apparently because the heat made outdoor worship more comfortable than gathering inside the building. After Clearview’s population declined, the church disbanded in the 1970s and joined with the Weleetka Church of Christ.

The building was later sold to a family and converted into a home. Sometime afterward, lightning struck the structure, and it burned to the ground.
